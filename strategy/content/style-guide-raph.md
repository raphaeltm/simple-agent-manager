# Raph's Writing Style Guide

**Derived from**: raphaeltm.com/posts (personal blog, 2021), ontech.raphaeltm.com (tech newsletter, 2025-2026)
**Last Updated**: 2026-03-15

## Voice

Raph writes like he's talking to a friend who happens to be interested in the same stuff. Conversational, first-person, thinking-out-loud. Confident in what he knows, honest about what he doesn't. He shares what he's built, what he's tried, and what he thinks — without hedging everything into mush or pretending to have all the answers.

Not a lecturer. Not a marketer. A builder writing about things he finds interesting.

## Tone Markers

- **Confident but not absolute**: states opinions and observations directly, qualifies only when genuinely uncertain — not as a reflex
- **Genuine enthusiasm without hype**: "I'm really excited about this release", "I really love standing by the bbq", "Man, did we have some wacky conversations"
- **Casual asides**: "see what I did there?", "pardon the pun", "to anyone wondering"
- **Honest about limits**: acknowledges what he doesn't know or hasn't figured out yet, but doesn't lead with it or make it the vibe

### What this is NOT

- **Not self-deprecating**. Don't open with "I'm pulling a blank" or "I don't really have much to say." That undercuts the writing before it starts. If you're writing about something, you have something to say — say it.
- **Not falsely modest**. "I'm no expert but…" before a clearly expert take reads as performative. Just make the point.
- **Not hedging everything**. "I think maybe possibly this could be…" — pick a position. It's fine to be wrong. It's not fine to be vague out of reflex.

## Sentence Structure

- Mixes short and long freely. Long, winding sentences that follow his train of thought, then a short punchy one to land a point.
- Frequent use of parenthetical asides within sentences (for context, caveats, or humor).
- Starts sentences with "And", "But", "So" — conversational connectors, not formal transitions.
- Uses questions addressed to the reader: "When was the last time you were excited about an OS update?"
- Sentence fragments are fine: "Awesome. Things ran much faster on it."

## Paragraph Style

- Paragraphs can be long — Raph doesn't break into tiny chunks for scannability. He writes in natural thought-blocks.
- Single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis or transition.
- No rigid structure — paragraphs flow where the thinking goes.

## Punctuation

- **Ellipses** (…): Used often, as thinking pauses. "So… what is this?", "Okay, so… I missed a couple days"
- **Em dashes** (—): Essentially never. Max 1 per blog post, none in social content. If you're reaching for an em dash, use a period, a parenthetical, or an ellipsis instead. Em dashes in Raph's published writing are almost certainly AI edits, not his voice.
- **Italics**: For emphasis on specific words, not whole phrases. "*literally*", "*have*", "kindof"
- **Quotes**: Around conceptual terms he's examining: "Content Creation", "creator economy", "artless"
- **Light on exclamation marks**: Uses them, but sparingly and genuinely — not for marketing energy.

## First Person

Always "I." Very present in the writing. Raph is the narrator. Personal experience is the entry point: "I remember meeting Lionello Lunesu in March 2022", "I build SAM from France", "Eventually I helped build the first version."

He also uses "we" naturally for collaborative work without making it feel corporate.

## Structural Patterns

- **No rigid template**. Posts don't follow hook→body→CTA formulas. They meander through the topic the way a conversation would.
- **Headers**: Used sparingly on the blog. The newsletter uses them more (section headers in longer pieces). Never overly formatted.
- **Lists**: Occasional, but not the backbone of the writing. Used when actually listing things (tools, features), not for scannability.
- **Code blocks**: Used when relevant, without over-explaining them. Trusts the reader to be technical.
- **Links**: Inline, casual. Not SEO-optimized anchor text — just natural references.

## Hooks / Openings

Almost always personal or situational:
- "I remember meeting Lionello Lunesu in March 2022."
- "People are having feelings about AI-generated imagery."
- "When was the last time you were excited about an OS update?"
- "Some of you might land here because you shared your email at some point..."

Never: "In today's fast-moving AI landscape..." or "Here's why X matters."
Never: self-deprecating openers that undercut the piece before it starts.

## Closings

Casual, often with a light touch:
- "Submit a task in French if you want. The agents don't mind."
- "Thanks for reading, or not reading, my word salad."
- "Those are my two, deeply intertwined tool selections for this episode of Tooltips. See you in the next one!"
- "If that sounds like it might be appealing to you, then stick around!"

Not salesy. Not urgent. Just wrapping up like you'd end a conversation.

## What He Avoids

- Marketing-speak: no "unlock", "leverage", "revolutionize", "game-changing"
- Structured authority: no "In this post, I will cover X, Y, and Z"
- Self-deprecation: no "I'm no expert", "I'm pulling a blank", "my word salad"
- Reflexive hedging: don't qualify every sentence with "I think maybe"
- Heavy formatting: no bold-everything, no emoji-heavy lists
- Clickbait: titles are descriptive or curious, not alarming

## What Makes It Feel Like Raph

1. **Personal stories as the way in**: even technical posts start with "I was doing X and then Y happened"
2. **Thinking out loud**: he works through ideas on the page, follows the thread where it goes
3. **Comfort with tangents**: the Monitors post wanders from multi-monitor setups to Apple ecosystem critique to iPad Pro appreciation — and that's fine
4. **Respects the reader's intelligence**: doesn't over-explain technical concepts, trusts context
5. **Confident and genuine**: enthusiasm is real, opinions are stated directly, uncertainty is acknowledged when real (not performed)
